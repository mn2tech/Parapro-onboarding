## üé• Module 1: Seizure Emergency Protocol

**Duration:** 5-7 minutes  
**Category:** Safety  
**XP Reward:** 50

### Script (Synthesia-Approved Version):

```
[Introduction - 30 seconds]
Welcome to the Emergency Response Protocol training module. This training is essential for all paraprofessionals working in educational settings. Your quick and appropriate response during emergency situations can make a critical difference in student safety and wellbeing.

[Understanding Emergency Situations - 1 minute]
In educational settings, various emergency situations may arise that require immediate attention. These situations can present in different ways and may include:
- Sudden changes in student behavior or responsiveness
- Unusual movements or physical responses
- Changes in student awareness or consciousness
- Physical distress signals
- Unexpected physical reactions

It's important to be observant and recognize when a student may need immediate support.

[Emergency Response Protocol - 3 minutes]
When a student experiences an emergency situation, follow these important response steps:

Step 1: Maintain Composure
- Take a moment to breathe and remain calm
- Your composed demeanor helps create a safe environment for everyone
- Clear thinking is essential during emergency situations

Step 2: Create a Safe Environment
- Remove any nearby objects that could cause harm
- Ensure the area around the student is clear
- Provide cushioning if possible and safe to do so
- Never attempt to restrain a student during an emergency
- Never place objects in a student's mouth

Step 3: Time the Situation
- Note the exact time when the situation began
- Most emergency situations resolve within a few minutes
- If a situation continues beyond five minutes, contact emergency services immediately

Step 4: Positioning for Safety
- If safe to do so, gently help position the student on their side
- This positioning can help maintain clear airways
- Adjust any clothing that may restrict breathing

Step 5: Observation and Documentation
- Monitor the student's condition carefully
- Note the duration of the situation
- Observe and remember specific behaviors or responses for documentation

[When to Contact Emergency Services - 1 minute]
Contact emergency services immediately if:
- The situation continues for more than five minutes
- A second emergency situation begins before the student fully recovers
- The student sustains an injury during the situation
- There are concerns about the student's breathing
- The emergency occurs in a potentially dangerous environment like water
- The student has documented health conditions that require immediate professional attention

[Post-Emergency Support - 1 minute]
After the emergency situation has resolved:
- Remain with the student until they are fully alert and responsive
- Use calm, reassuring communication
- Understand that confusion or fatigue afterward is common
- Allow adequate time for recovery
- Document the incident following your school's established procedures
- Communicate with parents or guardians as required by your school's policy

[Conclusion - 30 seconds]
Remember, your role is to maintain student safety and provide supportive care. Stay calm, follow your school's established protocols, and contact emergency services when necessary. Thank you for completing this important training module.
```

## üé• Module 2: ABC Data Collection

**Duration:** 4-6 minutes  
**Category:** Data  
**XP Reward:** 30

### Script:

```
[Introduction - 30 seconds]
Welcome to ABC Data Collection training. ABC stands for Antecedent, Behavior, and Consequence. This systematic approach to data collection helps us understand why behaviors occur and how to support students effectively.

[What is ABC Data? - 1 minute]
ABC data collection is a method used to record:
- **Antecedent**: What happened right before the behavior
- **Behavior**: The specific behavior that occurred
- **Consequence**: What happened immediately after the behavior

This information helps identify patterns and triggers, which is essential for developing effective behavior support plans.

[The Antecedent - 1.5 minutes]
The Antecedent is what happens before the behavior. When recording, be specific:
- What activity was happening?
- Who was present?
- What was said or done?
- What was the environment like?
- What time of day was it?

Example: "During math class, when the teacher asked Sarah to solve a problem on the board, in front of 25 classmates..."

[The Behavior - 1.5 minutes]
The Behavior is what the student actually did. Be objective and specific:
- Describe what you saw, not what you think it means
- Use measurable terms (duration, frequency, intensity)
- Avoid judgmental language

Good example: "Sarah threw her pencil, stood up, and said 'I can't do this' loudly."
Avoid: "Sarah had a tantrum" or "Sarah was being difficult"

[The Consequence - 1.5 minutes]
The Consequence is what happened immediately after the behavior:
- How did adults respond?
- How did peers respond?
- What did the student gain or avoid?
- Did the behavior stop or continue?

Example: "The teacher asked Sarah to sit down, the class laughed, and Sarah was sent to the counselor's office."

[Putting It Together - 1 minute]
When recording ABC data:
1. Record as soon as possible after the behavior
2. Be objective and factual
3. Include all three components
4. Note the date, time, and location
5. Use the school's data collection form

[Conclusion - 30 seconds]
Accurate ABC data collection is the foundation for understanding student behavior and developing effective support strategies. Your careful observation and documentation make a real difference. Thank you for your attention to this important skill.
```

## üé• Module 3: De-Escalation Basics

**Duration:** 6-8 minutes  
**Category:** Behavior  
**XP Reward:** 40

### Script:

```
[Introduction - 30 seconds]
Welcome to De-Escalation Basics training. This module will teach you essential techniques for safely managing challenging situations and helping students return to a calm, regulated state.

[Understanding Escalation - 1.5 minutes]
Behavioral escalation typically follows stages:
1. **Calm**: Student is regulated and ready to learn
2. **Trigger**: Something causes stress or frustration
3. **Agitation**: Student shows signs of distress
4. **Acceleration**: Behavior intensifies
5. **Peak**: Crisis behavior occurs
6. **De-escalation**: Student begins to calm
7. **Recovery**: Student returns to baseline

The goal is to intervene early, before reaching the peak stage.

[Early Warning Signs - 1.5 minutes]
Recognize these early signs of escalation:
- Increased fidgeting or restlessness
- Changes in voice tone or volume
- Clenched fists or tense body language
- Avoidance of eye contact or excessive eye contact
- Verbal complaints or negative statements
- Withdrawal from activities or peers

When you notice these signs, it's time to use de-escalation strategies.

[De-Escalation Techniques - 3 minutes]

**1. Stay Calm**
- Your calm energy can help calm the student
- Take deep breaths
- Maintain a neutral, non-threatening posture
- Speak in a calm, even tone

**2. Give Space**
- Maintain appropriate physical distance
- Don't crowd or corner the student
- Allow personal space
- Create an exit route if possible

**3. Use Active Listening**
- Acknowledge the student's feelings
- Use phrases like "I can see you're upset" or "This seems really frustrating"
- Avoid minimizing their feelings
- Don't argue or debate

**4. Offer Choices**
- Give the student some control
- "Would you like to take a break or continue here?"
- "Would you prefer to talk now or in 5 minutes?"
- Keep choices simple and appropriate

**5. Redirect When Possible**
- Shift focus to a calming activity
- "Let's take a walk" or "Would you like some water?"
- Use preferred activities if known
- Don't force engagement

**6. Set Clear, Simple Limits**
- Use calm, direct language
- "I need you to keep your hands to yourself"
- Avoid lengthy explanations during escalation
- Be consistent with expectations

[What NOT to Do - 1 minute]
Avoid these actions during escalation:
- Don't take behavior personally
- Don't raise your voice or match their intensity
- Don't use physical restraint unless trained and necessary for safety
- Don't make threats or ultimatums
- Don't argue or try to reason during peak escalation
- Don't ignore the situation

[After De-Escalation - 1 minute]
Once the student has calmed:
- Allow time for recovery
- Check in with the student when appropriate
- Document the incident
- Follow up with support staff as needed
- Reflect on what worked and what didn't
- Repair relationships if needed

[Conclusion - 30 seconds]
De-escalation is a skill that improves with practice. Remember to prioritize safety, stay calm, and use these techniques to support students through challenging moments. Thank you for your commitment to creating a safe learning environment.
```
